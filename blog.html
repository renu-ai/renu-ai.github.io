Rethinking “Statistical Significance” in Modern Research

March 2026

One idea that stuck with me this year came from reading Statistical Inference in the 21st Century: A World Beyond p < 0.05. The main argument is that science shouldn’t rely so heavily on a single cutoff like p < 0.05 to decide whether a result matters.

What surprised me is how arbitrary that threshold really is. A p-value of 0.049 is considered significant while 0.051 technically isn’t, even though the difference between those numbers is tiny.

Instead of focusing only on whether a p-value crosses a threshold, researchers should think more about the size of the effect, the uncertainty in the estimate, and how the result fits into the broader scientific context.

This idea also connects to the type of work I’m interested in. In machine learning and biomedical data analysis we often care more about predictive performance, robustness, and validation on new data rather than a single statistical cutoff.

As someone interested in applying AI to biomedical problems, this perspective feels especially relevant. Real clinical datasets are messy and complex, and meaningful insights rarely come from a single statistical test.

Moving beyond strict p-value thresholds encourages more thoughtful interpretation of results and, hopefully, more reliable science.
